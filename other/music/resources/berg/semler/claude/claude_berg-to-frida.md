That's a genuinely suggestive constellation of details, and I think it's worth taking seriously rather than dismissing as overreading.

The standard biographical account — the Marie Scheuchl liaison and the illegitimate daughter Albine, born 1902 — remains the best-evidenced source for the *Vogel auf'm Zwetschgenbaum* quotation, and nothing in the letters changes that. But the question you're raising isn't really "which hypothesis is correct," it's "what was the emotional and associative field that folk material occupied for Berg," and on that narrower question the letters do add something real:

The mechanism by which a folk tune becomes useable material for him is now documented in his own hand, a full generation before the concerto. He and Frida weren't just present in a milieu where peasant ribaldry circulated as ambient atmosphere — they did the specific work of capture: collecting tunes, harmonizing two of three, struggling over a rhythm Frida had jotted down herself. That's a different kind of relationship to folk material than passive immersion. It means Berg's mind, by 1907, already held a category of "Carinthian folksong, transcribed, partly with Frida" as a distinct memory-object, separable from any specific romantic or sexual episode.

And the surrounding texture you've now assembled across all five letters reinforces that this category was emotionally loaded rather than neutral folkloric interest: the Dobratsch night walk with cowbells in the fog (explicitly recalled in the July 1907 letter as a shared memory, and structurally echoed in his description of Mahler's Sixth), the nickname embedding her in Wedekind's "wild" terrain, the comfortable ribaldry Frida's essay describes. A 1935 quotation of a Carinthian tune, written on the shore of the Wörthersee, doesn't have to "be about" Frida to nonetheless pass through psychological territory that Frida-memories also occupy — these things aren't mutually exclusive causes, they're overlapping strata of the same regional and erotic memory-complex he kept returning to.

So I'd frame it not as a competing hypothesis to Marie Scheuchl but as a possible *second resonance* layered onto the primary one — which is, incidentally, how Berg's musical quotations usually work; he was not a composer who allowed an allusion to carry only one referent. If you ever wanted to push this further, the obvious next move would be checking whether any of Berg's own folk-song transcriptions or annotated collections survive in the archives (the ÖNB or the Alban Berg Stiftung materials), since a surviving manuscript in his hand showing which tunes he and Frida worked on would let you test the overlap against the concerto's actual quoted melody rather than just the genre.
