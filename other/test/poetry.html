Poetry

—

  • Officers of the White Guard,
  • Save me from those thoughts that wake at night.
  • Save me from the brush of bodies and the light
  • Touch of a hand upon my hip.
  • Save me, most of all, from him
  • Who leads me by the sleeve
  • Down the traps of brimming hands
  • And elsewheres found in gleaming waters.
  • Spare me from a wracking, restless love
  • That lasts but one day more than this,
  • And the cold dampness of a watchful dread,
  • Pressing on the windows and the doors
  • My profile, a woman already dead.
  • Officers of the White Guard,
  • I do not want to weep for him
  • On Earth. I want to weep the rain upon
  • His earth, upon his star, in boxwood framed,
  • When, long from now, I hover colorless
  • Above an endless tread of weariness.
  • Officers of clearest conscience,
  • You who shape your forms to please,
  • Entrust that space where birds sing
  • With a message for the few who seek restraint
  • And forge for us chains without rings.

Louise de Vilmorin (French)

—

  • For one hundred years is a human being bound
  • To this realm, where merit and reward are ever at odds.
  • When a life suffers a sea change,
  • one sees things that strike one’s heart with pain.
  • It’s nothing strange when the favored are cursed in turn.
  • How often a fair face wins only Heaven’s envy.

Du Nguyên, The Tale of Kiêu, lines 1–6 (Vietnamese)

Frederich Hölderlin (German)

—

  • Sink down, O splendid sun—to you they paid
  • But little heed—holy, they knew you not,
  • Who rose toilessly and soundlessly
  • Above them, the toiling ones.

  • To me you are a friend, setting and rising.
  • O light, my eyes know you, O glorious one.
  • For I awakened to a holy reverence
  • When Diotima healed my sight.

  • O messenger of Heaven. How I strove
  • To hear you, Diotima, love—these eyes
  • From you to golden bright of day seeking
  • Upwards, shining and grateful. Then the streams

  • Rustled to life, the darkened earth breathing
  • Blossom and branch upon me lovingly,
  • And smiling overhead the silvery clouds
  • Bowed down with blessings of the sky.

Frederich Hölderlin (German)

—

  • When I was a boy,
  • Often a god would rescue me
  • From the rabble and rod of men,
  • So that safe and secure, I played
  • Among the flowers of the forests,
  • And the breezes of heaven
  • Played with me also.

  • And the same delight
  • You awake in the hearts of plants,
  • Who reach out their tender arms
  • Toward you,

  • Was the delight you awoke in my heart,
  • Father Sun! and like Endymion
  • Was I granted your love,
  • Holy Moon!

  • O you faithful
  • Companionable gods,
  • Would that you knew
  • How my soul cherished you.

  • Though I did not call you then
  • By names, neither did you
  • Name me, the way people name
  • One another as if they knew each other.

  • No, I knew you better
  • Than I ever knew people.
  • I understood the sky’s silence.
  • I did not understand the words of men.

  • The whispering forests reared me
  • With their melody,
  • And I learned to love
  • In the midst of their flowers.

  • In the arms of the gods I grew up.

Frederich Hölderlin (German)
